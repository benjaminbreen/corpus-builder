PREFACE.

When we happened upon manuscript examples of the famous Oughtred, it seemed to us not a useless undertaking for beginners, nor ungrateful to those more practiced in mathematics, if we were to commit some of these to print. Wherefore, since some were incomplete, others not so suitable for the press, we excerpted only those which, on account of their use and variety, seemed not unworthy of public light and the name of Oughtred. Since he wrote most of these with the purpose of serving those he was instructing, young men cannot better initiate these studies than by accustoming themselves to Oughtred's genius. 

If anyone seems born for mathematics, let him take this as an example of a perspicacious mind and subtle judgment. Indeed, no one has used more perspicuous brevity, or more deeply embraced in his mind the very essences of things, which, while contemplating, he seems to create his own notions, and not so much discover truth as establish it. Of this matter, the [Clavis Mathematica] provides a great example.


[Clavis Mathematica], by accurate examination of matters and severity of judgment, so constantly accustoms the soul to rigid truth, that it can not only comprehend almost infinite things in one glance by the powers of imagination, but understand beyond the human measure, and anticipate the difficulty of discourse. Nowhere is a more sincere taste for demonstration drawn, or with equal fruit, is the mind's own agitation or skill advanced, nor is a more beautiful ordering of thoughts, or a more felicitous habit of congruent thinking, learned elsewhere. The chief utility of mathematics is a severe exercise of the mind; in every study, the love of truth, and the serenity born from the clarity of ideas diminishes that darkness as often as it descends to [sensibilia]. If mathematics does not serve human necessities, it becomes an idle adornment and is little esteemed from the uses of the human race. Those authors who labor with their excessive facility do an injustice to their readers, thinking that it is necessary for them to be instructed about absolutely everything; they present a method by which they understand the sun, entangled in a complex circuit, and wander from the correct path, theirs is the way of those who dwell in darkness. A mind avid for truth avoids superfluity so that it may attack the matter itself and test its powers; it seeks out difficulties and blushes to be wearied without victory. Oughtred did not wish to be easily understood; he deemed it sufficient that the nature of the human mind is such that it wants something left for itself to supply, so that it seems to learn from itself, and whatever aids it has used, the very invention of truth should be owed to itself alone.

Some treatises, which we present in a simple form, are owed to the labors of others, but reduced into compendium with greater utility for the readers. For the treasures which the authors themselves dug up, received polish and use from Oughtred, who never claimed anything of another, nor gloried in being the author, as if he could claim it as his own. In the [Institutiones Mechanicae], if anything is missing, it is abundantly supplied from the most accurate treatise of the famous Wallis, who alone treated this part of mathematics with such dignity, that anyone who might write about it afterward must borrow from him.

The center of gravity of any body is a certain point within it, having around it on all sides parts consisting of equal moments, according to which it is carried downward; and if the heavy body is suspended from this point, while it moves it remains at rest, preserving the position it had at the beginning. And that line is always perpendicular to the horizon.

A LIBRA [balance] is a straight line BAC, whose arms AB, AC are also distances, or segments of the balance from the center.

The center of the balance A, whether it is in the balance, or above or below, is always in a line perpendicular to the balance itself.

The TRUTINA [plumb line] is DA perpendicular to the horizon.

Prop. I. If a weight is sustained from the center of its gravity by the straight line A, it will not move unless that same line is perpendicular to the horizon.

Prop. II. A balance is equidistant to the horizon, whose center is above the balance, and having weights equal at the extremities, and equidistant from the perpendicular; if it is moved from such a position, it will return to the same and will remain there. For the center of gravity of a magnitude composed from the weights B C and the balance, is in E, wherefore (by I.) it is not sustained by the perpendicular AE; unless it is also perpendicular to the horizon.

For the center of gravity of a magnitude composed from the weights BC and the balance is E. Wherefore per I. &c.

To find the proportion of musical tones on a Cythara or Lute.

Let the line be divided according to the table above into tones E, F, F#, G, G#, A, B flat, &c. And taking the center at the beginning of the same line, draw arcs through single tones; then take with a compass the distance from the support to the edge on which the strings rest: and if it is a Cythara, note that distance in the last arc from the E tone; if it is a lute, in the fourth arc from the end, from the G tone, which is prefixed... let another line be drawn from the end to the center C, intersecting all the arcs. For the subtenses of the arcs themselves from E or G towards the center, intercepted between those two lines, will give the just distance of the tones from the support, but in a lute the tones towards the upper part of the neck must be raised a little, and that on account of the thickness of the strings, which determine the tones.

In Pneumatic Organs.

All pipes of pneumatic organs are constructed in the form of a cylinder: for that part which is from the top up to the lower lip of the opening, is cylindrical, and expresses the tone. Therefore those parts must be augmented or diminished according to the proportions of the line divided above.

Let it be given that I wish to construct another pipe from a given pipe, which shall be lower than it by 5 tones. Since the fifth tone differs from the first in a proportion of 800 to 1200, therefore, beginning from the lower lip of the opening, the desired pipe must be made one and a half times longer than the former.

On Bells.

Let a bell ABCD be given which I want to be similar to another that is lower in tone by a fourth: divide the line according to the table above into tones, let two arcs be drawn, one through E, the other through A, in which you measure... with a compass subtenses equal to the distances CD, AB, AC, EF: then from the center draw as many intersecting lines as there are arcs; and thus you have the dimensions of the bell to be made.

Of those things which, in any place of the munitions, have been erected, either advantage or detriment can result.

I. Advantages of munitions erected in a horizontal plain.

1. A rich and compact soil is suitable for the vallum, and the other structures

2. If light could be had, supplies could be brought at minimal expense.

3. Fertile cultivated soil supplies food.

4. If a stream should increase by too much quantity, either by cataracts, or by some other means, the surrounding fields can be flooded to the destruction of the enemy.

5. Such a site is well-suited for making tunnels, both on account of the wetness, and because the enemy digging can be seen from afar.

6. A regular form can be had.

7. It will be troublesome for the enemy to cover his troops so they are not seen.

Impediments.

1. The same soil will also be suitable for the enemy for erecting breastworks, munitions.

2. The river will also bring supplies to the enemy's forces.

3. The fertility of the fields will also serve the enemy.

5. A munition of this kind, unless a river obstructs, will be subject to attack from all sides.

7. A humble and depressed soil causes the valla or propugnacula to subside very often with great expense; on the refareienda [?] of which the greatest expenses are sometimes bestowed.

De the Munitions of Propugnacula.
7 *. Advantages of munitions next to a navigable ditch.

1. Access to the munition is difficult for diggers.

2. Though the enemy may hold the further shores, he cannot however hide himself in the ditch itself.

3. If he attempts to draw off the water; it will often deceive both the time & the labor.

4. Nor will he approach the vallum or the foundations of the propugnacula with his little muniments so as to secretly overturn them.

5. Unless the water is stagnant, whatever he may have thrown in for the sake of filling it, will fluctuate hither and thither.

6 But if the material is heavy, the depth of the ditch, so that it may be filled; will consume much time.
